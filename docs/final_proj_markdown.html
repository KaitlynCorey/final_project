Bar Graphs

Creates a graph showing the estimated percent of individuals with substance abuse disorder per state, in ascending order of occurance

ggplot(data=sub_use) +
  geom_col(aes(x=reorder(State, eighteen_or_older), y=eighteen_or_older)) +
  theme_minimal() +
  theme(axis.text.x = element_text(angle=90, size = 8)) +

Conclusion

Previous research as shown that individuals with serious mental illness often end up abusing substances as well (Comer). Based on the bar graphs shown, this does not seem to be the case at a state-wide level. When plotting the percent of individuals with substance abuse disorder per state and then the percent of individuals with a serious mental illness per state, one would expect the order of the states along the x axis to be the same (or at least very close) if these true values truly were correlated. However, this is not the case. The next graph created was a scatter plot. I expected to see a positive linear trend; however, this plot does not seem to follow any trend. The points do not seem to illustrate any sort of pattern. Finally, I calculated Pearson’s correlation. To indicate a significant relationship, the p-value would need to be below 0.05. For this data, the p-value was far above 0.05, at 0.217. These figures and this p-value all indicate that, with this data set, there is no significant relationship between the % of the state with a serious mental disorder and % of the state with substance abuse disorder. Perhaps if other factors were included, such as socioeconomic level, the relationship would become more signifiant. I would be interested to see what SAMHSA consinders to be a serious mental illness. Comer defines “serious mental illness” as bipolar disorder and schizophrenia, as individuals with these disorders often cannot hold a job. For this reason, I believe socioeconomic status may play a role.
